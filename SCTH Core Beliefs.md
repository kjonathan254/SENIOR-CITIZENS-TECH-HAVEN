# LAYER 1: GLOBAL FOUNDATIONS (20%)

## What Is Digital Exclusion?

### The Definition
Digital exclusion is the inability to access, use, or *benefit from* digital technologies and the internet. It is not merely the absence of a device or connection. It encompasses affordability, skills, confidence, motivation, accessibility, and—critically—the capacity to translate digital engagement into tangible outcomes.

### The Three-Level Model
Research has established that digital exclusion operates across three levels:

1. **First-level divide:** Gaps in physical access to devices and connectivity.
2. **Second-level divide:** Differences in digital skills, literacy, and usage patterns.
3. **Third-level divide:** Disparities in *outcomes* from internet use. Even when access and skills are equal, disadvantaged users derive fewer material benefits than privileged counterparts. This means the poor benefit least from digital engagement precisely when they need it most.

### The Social Nature of the Divide
Global evidence confirms that digital exclusion follows existing social fault lines. It is concentrated among low-income households, older adults, people with disabilities, racial and ethnic minorities, migrants, rural populations, and women. These patterns demonstrate that digital exclusion is not accidental but structurally embedded—it reproduces and amplifies pre-existing inequalities.

### Algorithmic Exclusion: A New Frontier
The emergence of AI has introduced a qualitative shift. "Algorithmic exclusion" occurs when AI systems lack sufficient data on marginalized individuals to return meaningful outputs, or when biased training data produces discriminatory outcomes. This creates "data deserts" where the digitally excluded are invisible to automated systems.

### Global Scale
As of 2024, 2.6 billion people remained offline globally. In low and lower-middle income countries, only 27% and 53% of populations have internet access, compared to 80% and 93% in upper-middle and high-income countries.

# LAYER 2: AFRICA (30%)

## How Does Digital Exclusion Differ in Emerging Economies?

### The Mobile-First Reality
Africa's digital economy is structurally different from the OECD context. In 2025, mobile technologies contributed $240 billion to Africa's economy (7.8% of GDP). Yet despite widespread network coverage, **almost 1 billion people in Africa are still not using mobile internet—representing 63% of the population**.

This reveals a critical African reality: the divide is not primarily about *coverage*. It is about **usage**—driven by device affordability, limited digital skills, and lack of relevant content in local languages.

### The Usage Gap vs. The Coverage Gap
In OECD contexts, digital exclusion debates often focus on broadband speed and fiber deployment. In Africa, the barrier is **getting people to use the services that already exist**. GSMA data shows that after years of infrastructure investment, the "usage gap" (people covered by networks but not using them) is the dominant challenge.

### Informal Support Networks as Infrastructure
In the absence of robust state social services, African digital inclusion relies heavily on informal support networks:
- **Family intermediation:** Children and grandchildren act as "digital brokers" for older adults.
- **Community Health Volunteers (CHVs):** In Kenya and similar contexts, CHVs serve as human bridges between digital health systems and older patients.
- **Agent banking:** Mobile money agents function as physical infrastructure in rural areas where banks do not exist.

This means digital exclusion in Africa cannot be solved with technology alone. It requires strengthening the *social* intermediaries that translate digital systems into human outcomes.

### Financial Inclusion as a Double-Edged Sword
M-Pesa in Kenya demonstrated that mobile financial services can achieve mass inclusion—79.4% of Kenyan adults used mobile financial services by 2019, up from 27.9% in 2009. However, this same success creates new forms of exclusion:
- **Cash float problems:** Rural agents struggle to maintain liquidity, forcing elderly customers to make repeated trips or wait for service.
- **Digital dependency:** As government cash transfers (like Inua Jamii) move to digital payments, those without digital literacy or mobile phones are structurally excluded from their own social protection benefits.

### The African Policy Context
The African Union and regional bodies have recognized digital transformation as essential, but implementation often outpaces citizen readiness. E-government is sometimes deployed "without sufficient consideration of local institutional capacities or citizen readiness, thereby leading to partial implementation with limited effects" and may "contribute to inequality if not addressed appropriately."

# LAYER 3: KENYA (50%)

## What Specifically Drives Digital Exclusion Among Older Adults in Kenya?

### The Demographic Reality
Kenya is experiencing rapid population aging alongside aggressive digital transformation. The government's Digital Health Strategy (2023–2027) positions digital tools as central to Universal Health Coverage (UHC). Yet the population of older adults affected by non-communicable diseases is growing—creating a direct collision between digital-by-default policy and analog-dependent citizens.

### The Data: How Exclusion Manifests in Kenya

#### 1. Internet Access Is Deeply Uneven
A 2025 joint survey by the Communications Authority of Kenya (CA) and Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) reveals stark disparities:
- **Nairobi:** 64.7% internet usage
- **West Pokot:** 9.1% internet usage
- **Turkana:** 12.7% internet usage
- **Tana River:** 15.5% internet usage

Only **11.6% of Kenyans aged 3+ use computers**, with just over 20% in urban areas. Smart home technology adoption remains limited outside high-income households.

#### 2. Digital Literacy Is Low and Age-Skewed
A national digital literacy assessment found that Kenya's ASAL (Arid and Semi-Arid Lands) counties have literacy rates below 50%. Samburu has the lowest at **27.56%**. Even in high-performing counties like Kiambu, the score is only **51%**.

Critically, government digital literacy training data shows that **only 1% of trainees are aged 71+, and just 5.7% are 56–60**. The vast majority (76.6%) are aged 18–45. This is not because older adults refuse to learn. It is because programs are designed for younger, urban, working-age populations.

#### 3. E-Government Actively Excludes Seniors
A 2025 study on e-government non-use among senior citizens in Kenya found that citizens aged 60+ encounter **"significant obstacles related to digital skill deficits, reduced technological confidence, and complex interface designs."**

The government's eCitizen and Huduma platforms—now mandatory for many services—assume digital literacy that older adults do not possess. When surveyed, only **51% of Kenyans felt e-services reached all citizens, including the elderly and disabled**. **36% said they did not.**

Respondents identified the top priority for improving digital services as **"digital literacy and public awareness" (72%)**, followed by "internet infrastructure and access" (56%) and "rural outreach/inclusion initiatives" (51%). The public itself recognizes that human factors outweigh technical ones.

#### 4. Digital Health Excludes the Elderly
A 2026 study in *PeerJ* analyzing over 500 patients (average age over 70) with chronic diseases in Kenya found that **"age is the strongest factor determining whether patients use mobile health tools."** Low digital literacy and limited confidence among older adults mean that as Kenya pushes digital health under UHC, older patients are being left behind.

The study concluded: **"Digital health should complement human care, not replace it."** It recommended investing in digital literacy for older adults using CHVs and primary care nurses as intermediaries—exactly the kind of social infrastructure that technology cannot replace.

#### 5. M-Pesa and Financial Exclusion
While M-Pesa revolutionized financial inclusion, it also created new barriers for older adults:
- **Interface complexity:** Menu-driven USSD systems assume literacy and numeracy.
- **Agent dependency:** Elderly users must travel to agents, who may have cash float shortages.
- **Trust deficits:** Older adults who did not use banks before M-Pesa often lack the confidence to manage mobile money.
- **Digital payment mandates:** As social protection programs like Inua Jamii shift to digital disbursement, older adults without phones or literacy are forced to rely on intermediaries—sometimes exposing them to financial abuse by caregivers or corrupt officials.

#### 6. The Informal Settlement Context
Research from Moi University on elderly health information practices in Kenya's informal settlements found that older adults adopt **"hybrid strategies that combine digital and traditional information sources, relying heavily on family members and community health volunteers as intermediaries."** Persistent barriers include low eHealth literacy, cost constraints, accessibility challenges, and trust concerns.

This confirms that even when technology is available, older adults in Kenya do not use it in isolation. They use it through *people*.

# SYNTHESIS: DERIVING SCTH'S CORE BELIEF

## From Three Layers of Evidence to One Thesis

### Global Layer Tells Us:
Digital exclusion is not about missing cables. It is about missing outcomes. The "third-level divide" proves that equal access and equal skills do not produce equal benefits when underlying social inequalities persist.

### African Layer Tells Us:
In emerging economies, the problem is not coverage but *usage*. The solutions that work—M-Pesa, mobile health, digital government—depend on informal social intermediaries (family, CHVs, agents) to function. Technology succeeds only when embedded in social infrastructure.

### Kenyan Layer Tells Us:
For older adults in Kenya, digital exclusion is manifesting in real-time through:
- E-Citizen platforms they cannot navigate
- M-Pesa systems they cannot operate independently
- Digital health tools they cannot use for their chronic conditions
- Literacy programs that systematically exclude them by age
- Social protection payments that require digital intermediaries they do not trust

In every case, the barrier is not the absence of technology. Kenya has fiber, 4G, M-Pesa, eCitizen, and digital health platforms. The barrier is the **absence of social scaffolding**—literacy training designed for elders, trusted human intermediaries, offline alternatives, and interfaces that respect cognitive aging.

## SCTH's Foundational Belief

> **Digital exclusion is a social issue, not just a technology issue.**

This is not a slogan. It is a thesis defended by evidence from three layers of analysis:

| Layer | Evidence for the Belief |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Global** | The third-level divide shows that access + skills ≠ outcomes. Social stratification determines who benefits. |
| **Africa** | 1 billion people are covered by mobile networks but not using them. The gap is social (affordability, literacy, relevance), not technical. |
| **Kenya** | Older adults are excluded from eCitizen, M-Pesa, and digital health not because the systems don't exist, but because they were designed without considering age, literacy, trust, and human intermediation. |

# STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR SCTH

### 1. Do Not Advocate for More Technology
Advocate for **technology wrapped in human support**. The evidence shows that older adults in Kenya use digital tools successfully only when CHVs, family members, or community agents act as bridges.

### 2. Demand Age-Responsive Design
Current digital literacy programs reach <1% of adults over 71. SCTH should push for training curricula designed for cognitive aging, not repurposed youth modules.

### 3. Protect Offline Channels
The "digital by default" approach in Kenya's e-government and health systems is actively excluding seniors. SCTH should advocate for **hybrid models** that maintain face-to-face alternatives.

### 4. Leverage Existing Social Infrastructure
Libraries, churches, community centers, and CHVs are already functioning as informal digital inclusion hubs. SCTH should invest in formalizing and funding these social intermediaries rather than building parallel tech solutions.

### 5. Frame Digital Exclusion as a Rights Issue
The UN has declared internet access a human right. In Kenya, where digital access is now required to receive social protection (Inua Jamii), access health services, and engage with government, digital exclusion is becoming a **barrier to citizenship itself**.
