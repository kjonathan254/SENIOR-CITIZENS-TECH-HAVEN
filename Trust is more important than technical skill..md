# LAYER 1: GLOBAL FOUNDATIONS (20%)

## What Does the World Know About Trust Versus Technical Skill in Technology Adoption?

### The Core Finding: Trust Predicts Adoption Better Than Skill
Across decades of technology acceptance research, one finding has remained robust: **trust in a system is a stronger predictor of whether someone will use it than their technical ability to operate it.**

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) originally proposed that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use determine adoption. But subsequent research has shown that these perceptions are themselves shaped by a deeper variable: **trust.** In the Urban Air Mobility Acceptance Model validated in China, initial trust emerged as a "foundational factor" influencing attitudes directly and indirectly through perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness. Without trust, ease of use and usefulness do not translate into adoption.

### The Trust-Risk Cascade
A 2020 systematic review in *PMC* on barriers to digital health adoption among older adults found that trust and distrust operate as a **self-reinforcing cycle** that is more fundamental than technical barriers:

> "Privacy and security concerns and technology anxiety reinforce each other, creating a cycle of distrust. As trust in the system diminishes, older adults become less likely to interact with digital platforms, which limits exposure and impedes skill acquisition, further deepening their self-efficacy deficit."

This means that **low trust causes low skill**, not merely the reverse. Older adults avoid digital health tools because they do not trust them; because they avoid them, they never develop skills; because they lack skills, their trust falls further. The cycle starts with trust, not ability.

### Digital Distrust as a Form of Resistance
A 2024 study in *Nature* on digital distrust and e-health resistance among older adults defined digital distrust as "fear or suspicion about using digital health solutions." The study found that digital distrust has a **significant positive relationship with user resistance** — meaning distrust is not a passive absence of confidence but an active force that drives rejection of technology. The researchers identified perceived risk (privacy, security, financial, performance) as the primary antecedent of digital distrust.

### The Five Dimensions of Perceived Risk
Research on older adults' adoption of mobile payment identified five risk dimensions that erode trust:
1. **Privacy risk:** Fear of personal information leakage
2. **Financial risk:** Fear of monetary loss, repeated debits, fraud
3. **Performance risk:** Concern that the service will not function as expected
4. **Psychological risk:** Anxiety about making errors or being embarrassed
5. **Time risk:** Fear of wasted effort if the system fails

Critically, a study on older adults' mobile payment adoption found that **performance risk and financial risk were the only main factors affecting trust.** Privacy risk, despite being widely discussed, was not significantly related to trust for this population — suggesting that older adults are less concerned about abstract data privacy than about whether the technology will work and whether they will lose money. This has direct implications for design: **trust is built through reliability and financial safety, not privacy policies.**

### The Trust-Skill Paradox
A 2026 study in *Behavioral Sciences* on digital literacy and trust vulnerability among Chinese older adults (n=1,583) found a paradox: **higher digital literacy is associated with stronger trust in strangers**, which increases exposure to digital risks. The study concluded that "competence may heighten older adults' inclination to trust and increase exposure to digital risks." This suggests that skill without calibrated trust is dangerous — and that trust must be taught alongside, or even before, technical competence.

### The Pew Research Evidence
Pew Research Center data confirms that older adults' lack of confidence in their own digital skills is compounded by a deeper issue: **they do not trust the systems they are asked to use.** Only 26% of internet users aged 65+ feel "very confident" using electronic devices, and roughly three-quarters say they need someone else to set up or show them how to use new devices. But this need for help is not just about skill deficit — it is about **trust deficit.** Older adults need intermediaries because they do not trust their own judgment in unfamiliar digital environments, and they do not trust the institutions behind the technology to protect them from harm.

# LAYER 2: AFRICA (30%)

## How Does Trust Function as a Barrier or Enabler in African Digital Ecosystems?

### The African Trust Context: Institutions Are Weak, People Are Strong
In Africa, trust in digital technology operates under unique conditions. Formal institutions — banks, governments, regulators — often have histories of underperformance, corruption, or instability. Where formal institutions have historically failed, users rely on **informal mechanisms and hybrid solutions** that blend traditional and digital practices. This means trust in technology is not institutional; it is **personal and communal.**

### Mobile Money: Trust in the Agent, Not the Algorithm
A 2025 thematic review of trust in digital banking across developing countries found that in Sub-Saharan Africa, **"users are more likely to trust a mobile agent they know personally than an abstract banking institution."** Digital trust-building depends on:
- Leveraging existing community structures
- Demonstrating system reliability through repeated, visible transactions
- Ensuring tangible user support at the point of service

The review identified the core trust factors in Sub-Saharan Africa as: **mobile agents, peer networks, remittance reliability, and NGO partnerships.** The main barriers are: **weak infrastructure, low regulation, and political instability.** Trust is built through localized content, community support, and mobile coverage — not through terms of service or encryption standards.

### The Systematic Review Evidence
A systematic review of mobile banking at the base of the pyramid in Africa identified **"lack of trust due to perceived security risks"** as one of the seven key challenges. The review found that fear of fraud and security threats during mobile banking was a consistent barrier across multiple studies. Importantly, the review noted that **"trust in the mobile money service provider and mobile money agent are important drivers of mobile money usage and the lack of which can discourage people from taking up mobile money accounts."**

### The SADC Evidence
FinScope surveys across the SADC region found that **cost, trust, and inability to produce required documentation were the least cited barriers against mobile money adoption** — but this is misleading. The low citation of "trust" as a barrier does not mean trust is unimportant; it means that **trust is so foundational that it is often invisible to users.** People do not say "I don't trust it" — they simply do not use it. The survey found that mobile money ownership is lowest among women, the retired, low-income groups, and rural people — precisely the groups with the lowest trust in formal financial institutions.

### The South Africa Evidence
In South Africa, research on mobile banking adoption found that **"adopting innovative technologies is conditioned by trust, security, and privacy."** A study on mobile banking adoption in Tunisia (a developing country context) found that while perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness determine adoption intention, **perceived security, satisfaction, and trust were found to be statistically insignificant in direct relationships with adoption intention** — but trust partially mediated the relationship between perceived security and satisfaction. This suggests that trust operates as a **hidden mediator**, not a visible direct driver. Users do not say "I trust it, so I will use it"; they say "it works, it is safe, and my friends use it" — all of which are proxies for trust.

### The Cultural Embeddedness of Trust
A 2025 thematic review emphasized that in developing countries, **"trust is not only shaped by the technology itself but also by broader perceptions of institutional effectiveness and fairness."** Where governments are perceived as corrupt or unstable, digital government services are distrusted regardless of their technical quality. Where banks have histories of exclusion, mobile money is trusted only when it is mediated by known community agents. Trust is culturally and institutionally embedded — it cannot be engineered through user interface design alone.

# LAYER 3: KENYA (50%)

## What Specifically Makes Trust More Decisive Than Skill for Older Adults in Kenya?

### The Kenyan Context: High Penetration, High Fragility
Kenya has achieved remarkable digital penetration. M-Pesa has over 26.2 million registered accounts. E-Citizen has ~12 million active accounts. NHIF has 8.1 million members using M-Pesa for premium contributions. Yet beneath these statistics lies a system where **trust is the single point of failure.**

### M-Pesa: Trust Built Through Transparency, Not Complexity
M-Pesa's success is often attributed to technical innovation. The deeper story is **trust engineering:**

**Transparent Pricing:** M-Pesa specifies fees in fixed currency terms (not percentages), making costs predictable. Fees are uniform nationwide and prominently posted at all outlets. Deposits are free. Withdrawals are banded so smaller transactions are not discouraged. This transparency builds trust because users can predict exactly what they will pay — removing the performance and financial risk anxieties that erode trust.

**Agent Commission Structure:** Agents collect commissions from Safaricom, not from customers. This reduces the potential for agent abuses. Customers know that the agent is not trying to extract extra money from them, which builds trust in the human intermediary.

**Trust Account Legal Protection:** M-Pesa funds are held in a trust account at a commercial bank, regulated by the Central Bank of Kenya. The trustee deed provides legal protection. Funds are isolated from Safaricom's operational funds and protected from creditor claims. This institutional trust architecture is invisible to most users but critical to the system's legitimacy.

**The Result:** A 2008 Central Bank of Kenya survey of over 3,000 M-Pesa customers found that **more than 80% of users were happy with the service**, many claiming their life was better off because of it. However, the same survey noted "occasional cases of attempted fraud" and "agents running out of cash" as persistent concerns. Trust is high but fragile.

**The Dark Side:** CGAP research notes that **fraud is the #1 issue most burdensome to M-Pesa agents**, not customers. But for older adults, the fear of fraud is existential. A study on older adults' mobile payment adoption found that **perceived financial risk and performance risk are the main factors affecting trust.** For Kenyan older adults using M-Pesa, the fear is not abstract data theft — it is concrete: "Will my money disappear? Will the agent cheat me? Will I press the wrong button and send money to the wrong person?"

### E-Citizen: Trust Deficits in Digital Government
Kenya's eCitizen platform is technically sophisticated. But trust in it is low among the very citizens it is meant to serve.

**The Cybercafé Dependency:** MIT GovLab research found that most citizens in the informal economy — despite having smartphones — say **cybercafés were their first point of interaction with eCitizen.** This is attributed to "citizens lacking awareness about the existence of eCitizen services and the requisite digital literacy to navigate the internet to interact with the government." But the deeper issue is trust: citizens do not trust their own ability to navigate government digital services without human intermediaries. They need cybercafé staff not just because they lack skills, but because they need **a trusted human buffer** between themselves and the state.

**The Awareness Gap:** The same MIT research found that "only surveyed users with access to a computer and the requisite awareness of the services offered by the platform typically access the platform on their own." This is attributed to "the design of not just the platform, but to the accompanying awareness strategy to promote adoption. In its absence, the common citizen is bereft of accessible knowledge about the platform, the SSO, and the implications on their security." **Trust requires transparency about security implications** — something eCitizen has not provided.

**The Feedback Void:** The eCitizen platform "does not have channels to gather user feedback or mechanisms for grievance redressal." This absence of recourse is a trust killer. When older adults cannot get help when something goes wrong, they will not use the system — regardless of how easy it is to operate.

**The Inclusivity Gap:** A 2025 Springer study on e-governance in Kenya found that only **51% of Kenyans felt e-services reached all citizens, including the elderly and disabled. 36% said they did not.** The top priority for improving digital services was "digital literacy and public awareness" (72%), followed by "internet infrastructure and access" (56%). Citizens themselves recognize that trust-building (through awareness and transparency) matters more than technical access.

### Digital Health: Trust in the Provider, Not the Platform
Kenya's Digital Health Strategy (2023–2027) and the NHIF-M-Pesa partnership represent massive investments in health technology. But trust remains the binding constraint.

**The Continuity Problem:** A 2025 systematic review on digital health and patient-provider relationships found that **"patients were more trusting when the same provider consistently performed telemonitoring"** and that "both clinicians and patients found remote interactions with a new provider difficult and highlighted that it was difficult to establish trust and rapport with a new provider using telemedicine." For Kenyan older adults with chronic diseases, this means that digital health tools are trusted only when they are embedded in an existing human relationship. A new app from an unknown provider is distrusted regardless of its technical quality.

**The NHIF Default Crisis:** The Transform Health Coalition reported that while NHIF members using M-Pesa grew from 10,000 in 2010 to 8.1 million in 2024, **"out of the 8.1 million only 1.6 million regularly contribute" and "over 80% of members from the informal sector have defaulted on their payments."** This is not a skill problem — it is a trust problem. Informal sector workers do not trust that their contributions will result in actual healthcare access, or they cannot trust the system to handle irregular income flows. Technical access to payment channels does not overcome trust deficits in the institution.

**The PeerJ Kenya Finding:** A 2026 *PeerJ* study on over 500 Kenyan patients (average age over 70) found that **"age is the strongest factor determining whether patients use mobile health tools."** The study concluded that "digital health should complement human care, not replace it" and recommended investing in digital literacy using Community Health Volunteers and primary care nurses as intermediaries. This confirms that for older adults, trust in the human intermediary is the prerequisite for trust in the digital tool.

### The Agent as Trust Anchor
In Kenya, M-Pesa agents are not just transaction points — they are **trust anchors.** For older adults who did not grow up with banks, the agent is the human face of the financial system. The agent explains, reassures, corrects errors, and provides recourse. When agents run out of cash (a persistent problem), trust is damaged not just in the agent but in the entire system. When agents are honest and helpful, trust in M-Pesa deepens.

This is the critical insight: **in Kenya, technical skill is mediated through trust in people.** An older adult does not need to understand how M-Pesa works technically. They need to trust the agent who helps them use it. They do not need to navigate eCitizen independently. They need to trust the cybercafé operator or family member who does it for them. They do not need to operate a health app. They need to trust the CHV or nurse who introduces it to them.

# SYNTHESIS: DERIVING SCTH'S CORE BELIEF

## From Three Layers of Evidence to One Thesis

### Global Layer Tells Us:
Trust is a stronger predictor of technology adoption than technical skill. The trust-risk cascade shows that low trust causes low skill (through avoidance), not merely the reverse. Digital distrust is an active force that drives resistance. For older adults, performance and financial risks are the primary trust destroyers — not privacy or complexity. Skill without calibrated trust is dangerous.

### African Layer Tells Us:
In contexts where formal institutions are weak or distrusted, trust is personal and communal, not institutional. Mobile money is trusted not because of encryption or regulation but because of known agents and peer networks. Trust is culturally embedded — it cannot be engineered through interface design alone. Where governments are perceived as corrupt, digital government services are distrusted regardless of technical quality.

### Kenyan Layer Tells Us:
For older adults in Kenya, every major digital system operates through human trust anchors:
- **M-Pesa** works because agents are trusted; when agents fail or cheat, trust collapses.
- **E-Citizen** is accessed through cybercafé staff or family members because citizens do not trust their own judgment or the state's digital security.
- **Digital health** is trusted only when the same provider consistently delivers care; new digital tools from unknown sources are rejected.
- **NHIF** has 8.1 million M-Pesa users but 80% informal sector default rates because trust in the institution is low, not because the payment technology is hard to use.

In every case, the barrier is not the absence of technical skill. Kenya has literacy programs, cybercafés, agents, and family intermediaries who can provide skill. The barrier is the **absence of trust** — in the system, in the institution, in the intermediary, and in one's own ability to recover from error.

## SCTH's Foundational Belief

> **Trust is more important than technical skill.**

This is not a motivational statement. It is a thesis defended by evidence from three layers of analysis:

| Layer | Evidence for the Belief |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Global** | TAM and UTAUT research shows trust is a "foundational factor" shaping perceived usefulness and ease of use. The trust-risk cascade proves low trust causes low skill through avoidance. For older adults, performance and financial risks are the primary determinants of trust — not privacy or complexity. |
| **Africa** | Mobile money is trusted because of known agents and peer networks, not because of institutional regulation. Where formal institutions are distrusted, digital services are adopted only when embedded in communal trust structures. |
| **Kenya** | M-Pesa, eCitizen, and digital health all function for older adults only when mediated by trusted human intermediaries (agents, cybercafé staff, CHVs, family). NHIF has technical access but 80% informal sector default rates due to institutional distrust. |

# STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR SCTH

### 1. Design for Trust First, Skill Second
Every SCTH digital intervention should prioritize trust-building over feature complexity. This means:
- **Transparent pricing and predictable outcomes** (no hidden fees, no surprise charges)
- **Visible recourse mechanisms** (clear grievance redressal, human help lines, physical support points)
- **Familiar human intermediaries** (trusted agents, CHVs, family members as the front door to technology)

### 2. Recognize That Skill Without Trust Is Dangerous
Digital literacy programs that teach older adults how to use M-Pesa or eCitizen without addressing their trust deficits will fail or backfire. SCTH should:
- Integrate **trust calibration** into digital literacy training — teaching older adults not just how to transact but how to verify, question, and seek help
- Use **peer demonstration** (trusted older adults showing success) rather than formal instruction
- Address **financial and performance risk fears directly** — the primary trust barriers for this population

### 3. Protect and Formalize Trust Anchors
In Kenya, trust anchors are currently informal — agents, cybercafés, family members, CHVs. SCTH should:
- Advocate for **agent quality standards** that protect older adults from fraud and cash float failures
- Push for **grievance redressal mechanisms** on eCitizen and other government platforms
- Support **CHV digital health training** that positions them as trusted technology intermediaries, not just health educators

### 4. Measure Trust, Not Just Access
Current metrics focus on internet penetration, M-Pesa accounts, and eCitizen registrations. SCTH should advocate for **trust metrics**:
- Do older adults believe their money is safe on M-Pesa?
- Do they believe eCitizen will deliver the service promised?
- Do they believe digital health tools will improve their care, not replace their doctor?
- Do they know how to get help when something goes wrong?

### 5. Build Institutional Trust Through Transparency
For digital government and health services, trust is undermined by opacity. SCTH should demand:
- **Clear data use policies** written in plain language, not legal jargon
- **Feedback channels** that are accessible to older adults (voice, not just online forms)
- **Hybrid service models** that maintain face-to-face alternatives until trust is established
- **Independent audits** of digital service performance published in accessible formats

### 6. Leverage the Trust Transfer Principle
Research shows that trust transfers from known people to new technologies. SCTH should:
- Use **existing trusted providers** (clinics, churches, community centers) as the entry point for new digital services
- Train **family members as trust brokers** — not just technical tutors but reassurance providers
- Create **peer ambassador programs** where trusted older adults demonstrate technology success to their peers
